# Academic Success in English Medium Instruction: A Literature Review

**Search basis:** Web of Science Core Collection, April 2026
**Query:** `TS=("English medium instruction" AND ("academic success" OR "academic achievement" OR "academic performance"))` — 105 results
**Scope:** Peer-reviewed empirical and review articles; sorted by citation count; supplemented with related works

---

## 1. Introduction

Somewhere between internationalisation ambition and classroom reality, a very practical question tends to get lost: do students actually learn better, worse, or about the same when their degree is taught in a language they did not grow up speaking? English Medium Instruction (EMI) has spread so rapidly across higher education in Europe, Asia, and the Middle East that institutions have, in many cases, made the policy decision long before the evidence base was ready to inform it (Macaro et al., 2018). The result is a field that is simultaneously urgent and uneven: urgent because hundreds of thousands of students are already sitting in EMI classrooms, uneven because the studies evaluating what that experience does to their learning are strikingly heterogeneous in design, context, and conclusions.

This review draws on 105 studies retrieved from the Web of Science Core Collection and organises the evidence around four questions: whether EMI students perform differently from peers taught in their first language; what role English proficiency plays in determining academic outcomes; how linguistic challenges unfold — and potentially recede — over the course of a programme; and what psychological factors shape achievement beyond language. The focus falls particularly on Turkish and East Asian university contexts, where EMI expansion has been both rapid and relatively well documented.

---

## 2. Comparative Academic Outcomes: EMI versus L1-Medium Programmes

The most direct way to evaluate EMI's effect on learning is to compare grades. The title of Dafouz et al.'s (2014) study — *'Surely they can't do as well'* — captures the prevailing intuition among sceptics: teaching complex subject matter in an additional language must, almost by definition, cost students something academically. The data, however, have been stubbornly uncooperative with that intuition.

Dafouz et al. (2014) tracked business students at a Spanish university across EMI and Spanish-medium cohorts and found no statistically significant difference in overall grades. What is perhaps more instructive is that they found no difference even in the students' own discipline — the content area they knew well — suggesting that the handicap, if it exists at all, is not primarily cognitive but linguistic, and that students can work around linguistic gaps when the subject matter is familiar. Dafouz and Camacho-Miñano (2016) pressed further in a longitudinal study of accounting students at the same institution. Their multi-year grade data told a more nuanced story: a small but real dip in the first year, followed by convergence with L1-medium peers by the second. Think of it as altitude sickness — uncomfortable at first, but something the body adjusts to once it acclimatises. The implication for institutions is important: a drop in first-year grades is not evidence that EMI is failing its students; it may simply be the cost of adjustment, and one that can be managed through early academic language support rather than by retreating from the model.

More recent evidence from Turkey reinforces this picture while complicating it at the edges. Cinkara and Yüksel (2025) examined medical and dental students' performance on national specialty board exams — high-stakes, standardised tests that are about as content-focused as assessments get. EMI students held their own across most specialties, though basic science subjects showed more divergence, likely because those fields lean heavily on technical vocabulary that sits at the intersection of language and knowledge in a way that, say, a clinical skills exam does not. Subject matter, in other words, is not a neutral variable: where language and disciplinary content are tightly intertwined, the EMI burden is heavier.

What these studies collectively suggest is that EMI does not automatically penalise academic achievement — but that claim needs a caveat. It tends to hold when students bring reasonable English proficiency to the table. When they do not, the comparisons look very different.

## 3. English Language Proficiency as a Predictor of Academic Success

If the medium of instruction does not automatically determine outcomes, what does? Among the variables that have been tested — motivation, prior academic performance, discipline, learning strategies — English proficiency tends to come out on top, and it does so consistently enough across contexts that the finding has become something close to a consensus (Macaro et al., 2018).

Xie and Curle (2020) put this to work in a mixed-methods study at a Chinese university, pairing questionnaire data and exam scores with semi-structured interviews across a hundred undergraduates. Students with stronger English profiles achieved more — that part was unsurprising. What the interviews added was texture: lower-proficiency students often understood the content well enough in their heads but struggled to access it through the language of instruction, like someone trying to read a book through frosted glass. Content knowledge was there; the medium was the obstacle.

In a Turkish context, Curle et al. (2020) asked a more pointed question: is it proficiency that matters, or is it simply that students educated in Turkish-medium secondary schools arrive less prepared for university in general? The regression analysis was fairly unambiguous. Proficiency predicted academic GPA; prior medium of schooling added little once proficiency was accounted for. This matters because Turkish EMI admissions policies have historically treated students from Turkish-medium schools as a risk category, sometimes directing them into preparatory language programmes regardless of their actual proficiency. The data suggest that is the wrong filter. What should matter at the gate is what a student can do in English, not where they went to school.

Proficiency, however, is not a uniform predictor across all levels. Aizawa et al. (2020), working with Japanese university students, identified what is best described as a threshold effect: below a certain proficiency level, even highly motivated and academically capable students ran into barriers they could not overcome through effort alone. Above the threshold, the picture changed — general English ability receded as the dominant predictor, and more specific academic language skills (reading dense disciplinary texts, following extended lectures, producing formal written arguments) became the differentiating factor. Proficiency, in other words, buys a student into the game; whether they win it depends on what they can do with language inside an academic discipline.

## 4. Linguistic Challenges and Language Development in EMI

Proficiency scores are, by their nature, snapshots. They tell us where a student stands at a single moment — typically before the programme begins — but say nothing about what happens to language as the academic year unfolds. The more interesting question, and one that has received growing attention, is whether EMI itself changes students' linguistic capabilities, and whether those changes feed back into academic performance.

The answer, from Turkish EMI data, is that they do — but not uniformly. Soruç et al. (2021) surveyed students across a Turkish EMI institution and found that listening comprehension and academic writing were the most consistently reported sites of difficulty, particularly for lower-proficiency entrants. Neither is surprising — lecture-following and formal writing are genuinely hard in a second language — but what matters is the trajectory: students who made measurable English gains over the academic year reported significantly fewer difficulties by its end. The programme was, in effect, doing some of the preparatory work that prior schooling had not.

Yuksel et al. (2021) traced this dynamic longitudinally, tracking proficiency gains alongside GPA across a full academic year at a Turkish university. The two moved together, and the relationship grew stronger as the year progressed rather than levelling off — as if, past a certain point, language and learning start reinforcing each other in a way that simply was not available when both were being built from scratch. Most practically, students who arrived with relatively low proficiency still achieved satisfactory academic outcomes when their language improved enough during the year. That should give pause to any institution that treats low entry proficiency as grounds for outright exclusion from EMI; the evidence suggests the situation is more dynamic — and more amenable to intervention — than a single admission test implies.

## 5. Psychological and Motivational Determinants of EMI Success

Language proficiency matters, but it does not explain everything. Two students can arrive at an EMI programme with near-identical proficiency scores and diverge significantly in academic outcome — a gap that proficiency alone cannot account for. Research has increasingly turned to the psychological space between what students *can* do in English and what they actually *do* with it in academic settings.

Guan et al. (2025) tested this through structural equation modelling among Chinese EMI students, combining self-actualization, language self-efficacy, and academic emotions in a single predictive model. Language self-efficacy — confidence specifically in one's capacity to function in English academically — emerged as a mediator between broader self-belief and achievement. It is not simply that confident students do better, but that confidence in the linguistic domain acts as a gateway: it shapes whether students lean into the EMI environment or hold it at a wary distance. Positive academic emotions had a direct positive effect on performance even after proficiency was held constant; anxiety suppressed achievement through a channel that ran independently of language ability. A student can follow a lecture perfectly well and still underperform on the exam if a good part of their attention was occupied by the effort of not appearing lost.

Motivational orientation tells a related story. Xie and Curle (2020) found that students who saw EMI as an opportunity rather than an imposition achieved more than those who were simply complying with an institutional decision. Policymakers rarely account for this: converting a programme to English changes the medium, but it does not automatically generate the orientation toward English that would make the switch educationally effective. Students need reasons to engage with the language, and those reasons are not usually produced by a curriculum decision alone.

## 6. Research Landscape and What Remains Open

Macaro et al.'s (2018) systematic review of 83 EMI studies reads, in places, like a field taking stock of how much it does not yet know. The 83 studies vary enormously in how they define EMI (from fully English-taught programmes to single bilingual courses), in what they mean by academic success (grades, test scores, self-reported learning, lecturer evaluations), and in the research designs they use to pursue the question. Most are small-scale and cross-sectional; very few follow students over more than one semester. The review is, in that sense, an honest map of a territory that remains partially uncharted — useful precisely because it does not flatten the complexity into tidy conclusions.

What has accumulated since reads more like promising reconnaissance than settled understanding. The Turkish context, in particular, has produced a cluster of methodologically careful studies (Curle et al., 2020; Soruç et al., 2021; Yuksel et al., 2021; Cinkara & Yüksel, 2025) that offer a more granular picture than most single-country datasets, sharing a willingness to track students over time and to treat language development as a dynamic variable rather than a fixed admission condition.

Two gaps remain. Most EMI achievement research has been conducted in business and economics; healthcare, engineering, and the natural sciences — fields that make up a large proportion of actual EMI enrolments — remain comparatively understudied despite presenting their own specific linguistic demands. More broadly, most studies isolate a single category of predictor rather than testing how linguistic, motivational, and contextual variables interact. Academic outcomes in EMI are shaped by all of these in combination, and research designs able to capture that interplay are still the exception rather than the rule.

---

## 7. Conclusion

The question that opened this review turns out not to have a single answer — which is perhaps more honest than the field sometimes acknowledges. Grade comparisons suggest that, on average, academic performance does not suffer significantly under EMI. But that average conceals a distribution: students with strong proficiency, high self-efficacy, and genuine motivation at one end, and students who spent the year managing a linguistic burden that displaced energy that should have gone into learning at the other.

English proficiency is the most consistently predictive variable, but it is not a destiny. Longitudinal data from Turkish universities show that language development and academic achievement can move in tandem during a programme — students do not have to arrive fully prepared to finish that way, provided the institution treats language as a live concern throughout rather than a matter settled at admission. Self-efficacy and motivation shape what students do with whatever proficiency they bring, which means that better EMI outcomes cannot be engineered through language testing alone.

What this body of research most clearly implies is a different way of thinking about the EMI student: not as someone who either clears the proficiency bar or does not, but as someone whose academic and linguistic trajectories are intertwined in ways a programme can choose to support — or simply ignore.
