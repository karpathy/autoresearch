## Abstract

English-medium instruction (EMI) in Türkiye has expanded alongside internationalisation, yet limited evidence shows whether EMI *replicates* or *reshapes* access for diverse students relative to Turkish-medium instruction (TMI). Framed by equity, diversity, and inclusion (EDI), this explanatory sequential mixed-methods study analyses nationally standardised placement data (YÖK Atlas, 2023–2025) for undergraduate EMI and TMI programmes, excluding language-focused degrees to preserve a content-EMI definition. Indicators are gender, disability among placed students, and provincial socioeconomic tier (SEGE-2025), with tier-level 15–24 population denominators (TURKSTAT). Quantitatively, EMI intakes were near gender parity while TMI showed a stable female majority; disability representation was very low in both tracks and lower in EMI throughout; EMI placements concentrated in the highest socioeconomic tier, with weaker population-adjusted reach in lower tiers than TMI. A qualitative strand (semi-structured interviews) will interpret how students narrate access. The study contributes national, placement-level evidence on who enters EMI versus TMI—complementing classroom- and achievement-focused EMI research—and discusses implications for monitoring, widening participation, and inclusive EMI policy.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem and rationale

EMI is frequently justified through competitiveness, employability, and research connectivity (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). Those rationales intersect with **distributional** questions: EMI can function as *gatekeeping* when English proficiency, examinations, private preparation, and geography sort students before coursework begins (Piller, 2016; Macaro et al., 2018). EMI is therefore not only pedagogy but a **structuring feature of opportunity** in systems where the national language remains the default medium for most degrees.

In Türkiye, programme-level EMI growth and uneven disciplinary spread coexist with debates on internationalisation, prestige, and Turkish-medium provision (Altay, 2025; Curle et al., 2020). What remains scarce for strong empirical claims—especially at national scale—is evidence on whether **newly placed** EMI cohorts mirror the demographic and socioeconomic composition of TMI, the dominant undergraduate track. Achievement and classroom-process studies often bracket *who gains placement*; without placement-level profiles, EDI arguments risk resting on institutional anecdotes or small-N designs.

### 1.2 Aims and contribution

This study asks how profiles of newly placed undergraduates in EMI and TMI differ (2023–2025) on gender, disability, and socioeconomic tier, and (qualitatively) how students describe access. Its contribution is threefold: **(1)** national **placement demography** contrasting EMI with TMI; **(2)** explicit attention to **disability representation** alongside gender and geography; **(3)** interpretation of tiered outcomes using **population denominators**, reducing misreading of raw counts. The design aligns with explanatory sequential mixed methods: quantify patterns, then interpret mechanisms (Heigham & Croker, 2009; Riazi, 2016).

## 2. Literature review: Equity, diversity, and inclusion in EMI

### 2.1 EMI as internationalisation—and as a site of justice claims

Systematic reviews document heterogeneous EMI implementation, stakeholder concerns about readiness and support, and uneven evidence on language and content outcomes (Macaro et al., 2018). Sociolinguistically, EMI participates in struggles over **linguistic justice**: which languages legitimate academic work, who bears the costs of Englishisation, and whether multilingual repertoires are recognised (Piller, 2016; Soler, 2024). Critical policy perspectives ask how EMI may **amplify inequality** when English-related resources are unequally distributed (Piller, 2016; Alhamami, 2023). A growing **critical EMI** line of work examines how Englishisation interacts with power, pedagogy, and institutional practice in higher education (Wang, 2025), providing a useful counterpoint to purely instrumental internationalisation narratives.

For this paper, **equity** refers to fair access to opportunity structures (preparation, admissions, placement), not merely formal equality of rules. **Diversity** is operationalised through cohort composition on gender and recorded disability—acknowledging that intersectionality and non-binary gender are only partially visible in administrative data. **Inclusion** is examined at **entry**: whether instructional tracks differ in who is represented among placed students, before in-course accommodation is considered.

### 2.2 Language rights, inequity, and EMI programmes

Empirical and conceptual work on EMI has increasingly connected medium-of-instruction policy to **language rights** and structural inequity for users of English as an additional language (Alhamami, 2023). That line of research supports examining EMI not only as curriculum but as a **policy environment** that may constrain participation when proficiency demands, assessment formats, and information asymmetries interact. It motivates attention to **disability** as well: language-intensive gatekeeping can compound barriers unless admissions, preparatory provision, and accommodations are aligned (see inclusive EAP and higher-education inclusion literature more broadly).

### 2.3 Access, proficiency, and the Turkish context

EMI selection often correlates with English proficiency and prior schooling tracks associated with advantage (Macaro et al., 2018). In Türkiye, proficiency predicts EMI academic success, highlighting entry thresholds and ongoing support (Curle et al., 2020). Longitudinal evidence shows language development and achievement can co-develop during programmes (Yüksel et al., 2021)—relevant *after* admission but not substituting for questions about *who is admitted*. National-level analysis of **which provinces and social geographies** feed EMI placement connects to work on **linguistic protectionism** and disciplinary patterns of EMI supply (Altay, 2025).

### 2.4 Gender, disability, and spatial stratification

Gendered participation patterns in higher education are well documented globally; the EMI-specific question is whether EMI and national-medium tracks show **different balances**, reflecting programme mix, signalling, or self-selection. **Disability** representation in EMI remains under-reported in large-scale comparisons; administrative counts are imperfect (registration, disclosure, categories) but necessary to surface scale-level disparities. **Socioeconomic geography**: provincial development indices capture multidimensional stratification better than a single income proxy; combining tiers with youth **population bases** avoids interpreting large raw counts in populous regions as equivalent “demand” to smaller regions.

### 2.5 Multilingual and justice-oriented responses (context for discussion)

At the level of **language policy and planning**, research on EMI in higher education has articulated pathways toward **multilingual** rather than monolingually English-only models, including deliberate planning for heterogeneous student repertoires in English-medium classrooms (Dafouz & Smit, 2022; Ou et al., 2022). These perspectives complement **ELF-aware** and justice-oriented arguments that question accent hierarchies and call for inclusive regimes of listening and uptake (Soler, 2024; see also Macaro et al., 2018, on implementation variability). None of this removes the need to study **who reaches EMI in the first place**; it does, however, supply a policy vocabulary—multilingual planning, support, assessment fairness—for the **discussion** of what might accompany widening participation in Türkiye’s EMI expansion.

### 2.6 Synthesis

EMI equity research would *expect* differentiated intakes when English resources and preparatory opportunity are unequally distributed (Piller, 2016; Macaro et al., 2018; Curle et al., 2020; Alhamami, 2023). Critical and multilingual-policy perspectives further suggest that EMI should be analysed as both **access structure** and **pedagogical–ideological project** (Wang, 2025; Dafouz & Smit, 2022). Whether Türkiye’s **central placement system** produces systematic EMI–TMI contrasts on gender, disability, and SEGE-tier concentration at national scale is **empirical**—and central to this study.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Research questions

**RQ1.** How did the profile of newly placed undergraduate students in EMI and TMI programmes in Türkiye differ across the 2023–2025 admission cycles in terms of:  
**(a)** gender, **(b)** disability, and **(c)** socioeconomic tier?

**RQ2.** How do students describe the role of gender, disability, and socioeconomic background in shaping access to EMI and TMI programmes in Türkiye?

### 3.2 Overview and design

This study examines EDI in Turkish higher education by comparing newly placed undergraduates in EMI and TMI across 2023–2025 on gender, disability, and socioeconomic tier. TMI serves as the **comparative baseline** for evaluating the inclusiveness of EMI at placement stage.

An **explanatory sequential mixed-methods design** was adopted: quantitative analysis of national placements, followed by qualitative interviews (Heigham & Croker, 2009; Riazi, 2016). RQ1 requires descriptive and comparative analysis at national scale; RQ2 requires interpretive exploration of how students understand access. The quantitative phase establishes distributional patterns; the qualitative phase adds explanatory depth.

### 3.3 Context of the study

EMI has expanded worldwide and is tied to internationalisation and competitiveness (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). Scholarship increasingly addresses access, participation, and justice—not only language proficiency (Piller, 2016; Soler, 2024; Alhamami, 2023). In Türkiye, EMI growth and disciplinary unevenness make it important to study EMI beyond prestige framings, including access and representation (Altay, 2025; Curle et al., 2020). Because TMI remains the dominant undergraduate mode, comparing tracks supports a social-justice reading of placement outcomes.

### 3.4 Participants

The quantitative phase did not recruit individuals; the unit of analysis was **newly placed undergraduate students** in EMI and TMI nationwide (2023–2025 cycles).

The qualitative phase involves undergraduates in EMI and TMI. **Purposeful sampling** sought variation on programme medium, and where feasible on gender, socioeconomic background, and disability-related experience, to elicit perspectives on programme choice, placement, and perceived opportunity structures (Dörnyei, 2001). Sampling continued toward **thematic saturation** (Dörnyei, 2001).

### 3.5 Ethics

The qualitative component followed institutional ethics review **[insert protocol / approval number]**. Participants gave **informed consent**; interviews were audio-recorded; data were stored securely; confidentiality and the right to withdraw were honoured. The quantitative analysis used **aggregated public administrative data** (YÖK Atlas; SEGE-2025; TURKSTAT); no individual student records were analysed beyond published tabulations.

### 3.6 Data collection

**Quantitative.** Data came from **YÖK Atlas** (Turkish Council of Higher Education), covering programmes, institutions, and placements. Records were compiled for 2023–2025 and split by undergraduate EMI versus TMI.

**EMI definition and exclusions.** Language-focused programmes were excluded so EMI denotes **content** instruction through English, not language teaching *per se* (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). Excluded areas included English language studies, foreign language education, translation/interpreting, linguistics, and cognate language-centred fields. **Residual misclassification** (e.g., hybrid labels) was minimised through manual checks where labels were ambiguous; a small number of records with incomplete key fields were omitted.

**Indicators.** (1) Gender distribution of placed students. (2) Counts and shares of placed students recorded as disabled. (3) Socioeconomic geography via **SEGE-2025** (Ministry of Industry and Technology): six tiers from 52 variables across eight themes. **Denominators:** provincial 15–24 populations from **TURKSTAT** (15–19 and 20–24 combined) were aggregated within each tier so placement counts could be read against an approximate **candidate-age pool**.

**Qualitative.** **Semi-structured interviews** (Turkish or English per participant preference) addressed how students understand gender, disability, and socioeconomic background in access to EMI/TMI, plus barriers, fairness, family influence, and meanings of each track (Heigham & Croker, 2009). Transcripts were produced for analysis.

### 3.7 Analysis

**Quantitative.** **Descriptive statistics** and comparative tabulations (frequencies, percentages by year and track) addressed RQ1 without causal inference claims (Agresti & Franklin, 2014; Field, 2013). Tier analysis used both absolute placements and **placements relative to tier 15–24 population**. Visualisations supported longitudinal comparison.

**Interpretive boundaries.** Findings reflect a **curated** national dataset comparable across EMI and TMI, not an unfiltered dump of all programmes. **SEGE tier is ecological**, not student household SES. **Disability** figures depend on administrative categories and disclosure. **Gender** follows binary registry fields. **Denominators** approximate, but do not perfectly map, the examination-taking population.

**Qualitative.** **Thematic analysis** followed Braun and Clarke’s (2006) phases: familiarisation, inductive coding, theme construction around access, inclusion, exclusion, and structure, and iterative validation against transcripts.

**Integration.** In the final manuscript, qualitative results will **connect** national patterns to student sense-making (Heigham & Croker, 2009).

## 4. Findings

*The following presents the quantitative results for RQ1. Qualitative results for RQ2 will be added in a later revision.*

### 4.1 Gender distribution across EMI and TMI

Table 1 summarises gender and disability for newly placed students by track and year. **Gender** showed a stable contrast: **EMI** remained close to parity (female 50.56%, 50.81%, 50.71% for 2023–2025; male 49.44%, 49.19%, 49.29%). **TMI** showed a persistent female majority (female 58.95%, 59.20%, 58.83%; male 41.05%, 40.80%, 41.17%). Total placements grew in both tracks (EMI 91,053 → 104,770; TMI 427,274 → 492,179), yet **gender profiles were stable**. **RQ1(a):** EMI and TMI differed consistently: EMI near balance, TMI female-majority.

### 4.2 Newly placed disabled students

Shares were very small in both tracks but **higher in TMI** each year. **EMI:** 66 (0.072%), 72 (0.076%), 77 (0.073%). **TMI:** 446 (0.104%), 460 (0.102%), 476 (0.097%). The gap was modest in percentage points but **persistent**. **RQ1(b):** Recorded disability representation was low overall and lower in EMI than TMI across 2023–2025.

### 4.3 Socioeconomic tiers

Provinces were grouped with **SEGE-2025**; tier populations (15–24) contextualised placements (full tier table: Appendix; Figure 1: summary). **EMI** was **concentrated in Tier 1** (highest development): e.g. 55,905 of 91,053 EMI placements in 2023 (≈61%), rising to 66,648 of 104,770 in 2025—roughly **three-fifths** of EMI from the top tier throughout. **TMI** also had the largest absolute count in Tier 1 but was **more dispersed** across tiers.

Lower tiers illustrated the contrast. **EMI Tier 6** placements: 2,810; 2,892; 3,138—**0.14–0.16%** of Tier 6’s 15–24 pool. **TMI Tier 6:** 54,700; 53,049; 66,180. In 2025, **EMI** placement rates by tier population declined from Tier 1 (1.30%) through Tier 6 (0.16%); **TMI** showed a flatter pattern (e.g. 4.38% Tier 4, 4.23% Tier 5, 3.86% Tier 1), reflecting broader geographic spread.

**Stability:** 2023–2025 showed **continuity**, not structural convergence: Tier 1 remained the hub of EMI; TMI stayed more evenly spread. **RQ1(c):** EMI and TMI differed systematically in socioeconomic geography; EMI was disproportionately Tier 1–oriented with limited population-adjusted reach in lower tiers.

### 4.4 Summary of quantitative findings

EMI and TMI were not interchangeable by intake profile: **more gender-balanced EMI** versus **female-majority TMI**; **lower recorded disability share in EMI**; **strong EMI concentration in the highest SEGE tier** versus **more dispersed TMI**. Together, these patterns are directly relevant to EDI monitoring in Turkish higher education.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Interpreting the EMI–TMI contrast

**Stability under expansion.** Despite growing placement volumes, **disability and socioeconomic contrasts** persisted. That aligns with treating EMI as part of a **sorting infrastructure** shaped by proficiency, preparation, geography, and programme supply—not as a neutral relabelling of the same intake (Piller, 2016; Macaro et al., 2018; Alhamami, 2023).

**Gender.** EMI’s **near parity** versus TMI’s **female majority** suggests **different selection into tracks**. Interpretation should remain cautious: parity is not automatic fairness for all subgroups; differences may reflect **field mix** (EMI clustering in certain disciplines), signalling, regional supply, or applicant preferences. Discipline-disaggregated extensions would sharpen interpretation.

**Disability.** Smaller **recorded** disability shares in EMI warrant policy attention: English entry demands, pace, assessment formats, and accommodation pipelines may interact with disclosure and support (see language-rights and inclusion framings; Alhamami, 2023). Qualitative data can clarify mechanisms (information, stigma, reasonable adjustment awareness).

**Socioeconomics.** Tier-1 concentration of EMI—with low population-adjusted EMI rates in Tier 6—is consistent with **spatial stratification** of opportunity: students in less-developed regions face fewer local EMI places or heavier cumulative barriers to placement. This resonates with sociolinguistic and policy accounts of EMI as **resource-intensive** (Piller, 2016; Altay, 2025) and with calls to monitor **access structures**, not only learning outcomes.

### 5.2 Relation to the literature

Much EMI research is **institutional** or **achievement-focused** (Macaro et al., 2018; Curle et al., 2020; Yüksel et al., 2021). This study adds **national placement demography** contrasting EMI with **L1-medium** mass provision—a gap in many reviews. It also foregrounds **disability** at scale. The SEGE tier + denominator approach offers a **transparent geographic equity lens** for Türkiye. **Linguistic justice** and **critical EMI** perspectives (Soler, 2024; Wang, 2025) support discussing not only “more English support” but **who bears the costs** of Englishisation; **multilingual planning** arguments (Dafouz & Smit, 2022; Ou et al., 2022) connect those costs to classroom- and policy-level responses—without presuming causality from ecological placement data.

### 5.3 Limitations and future research

Ecological SES; binary gender in source data; disability **under-reporting**; EMI/TMI **field composition** not separated here; no individual English scores in YÖK Atlas extracts. Future work: **field-stratified** or multilevel models; linkage to **preparatory-year** pathways; qualitative integration for mechanisms; trend analysis beyond three cycles.

## 6. Conclusion

Between 2023 and 2025, newly placed students in **EMI** and **TMI** in Türkiye differed markedly in **gender balance**, **recorded disability representation**, and **socioeconomic geography**. EMI approached gender parity; TMI stayed female-majority. Disability shares were very small in both tracks and consistently lower in EMI. EMI placements clustered in the highest SEGE tier with limited population-adjusted presence in the lowest tier; TMI spread more broadly. These findings describe **who gains access** to each track at national scale and complement classroom- and proficiency-oriented EMI research (Macaro et al., 2018; Curle et al., 2020; Yüksel et al., 2021).

Contributions include **placement-level EDI evidence** for a major non-Anglophone system, **explicit disability visibility**, and a **replicable geographic method** (SEGE tiers with youth denominators). Framed by linguistic justice and language-rights concerns (Piller, 2016; Soler, 2024; Alhamami, 2023), the results support **monitoring inclusion** as EMI expands (Altay, 2025). Upcoming qualitative analysis will connect distributions to student meanings of fairness and constraint (Heigham & Croker, 2009; Riazi, 2016).

**Implications (hypothesis-generating):** widen EMI-related **outreach and preparation** in lower-tier regions; review **English-intensive gatekeeping** and **disability-inclusive** admissions and support; maintain **gender-aware** guidance where TMI pathways are highly feminised. Evidence on **what works** should combine these structural indicators with evaluation of specific interventions.
