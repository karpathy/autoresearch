# Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion in English- and Turkish-Medium Undergraduate Placements in Türkiye: National Evidence from Administrative Data (2023–2025)

*Draft journal article. Target length including references, tables, and abstract: approximately 8,000 words or less.*

---

## Abstract

English-medium instruction (EMI) in Türkiye has expanded alongside internationalisation, yet limited evidence shows whether EMI *replicates* or *reshapes* access for diverse students relative to Turkish-medium instruction (TMI). Framed by equity, diversity, and inclusion (EDI), this study analyses nationally standardised placement data (YÖK Atlas, 2023–2025) for undergraduate EMI and TMI programmes, excluding language-focused degrees to preserve a content-EMI definition. Indicators are gender, disability among placed students, and provincial socioeconomic tier (SEGE-2025), with tier-level 15–24 population denominators (TURKSTAT). EMI intakes were near gender parity while TMI showed a stable female majority; disability representation was very low in both tracks and lower in EMI throughout; EMI placements concentrated in the highest socioeconomic tier, with weaker population-adjusted reach in lower tiers than TMI. The article contributes national, placement-level evidence on who enters EMI versus TMI—complementing classroom- and achievement-focused EMI research—and discusses implications for monitoring, widening participation, and inclusive EMI policy. Complementary qualitative analysis of student accounts is reported as it becomes available.

**Keywords:** English-medium instruction; Turkish-medium instruction; equity; inclusion; disability; socioeconomic development; Türkiye; higher education placement; administrative data

## 1. Introduction

English-medium instruction (EMI) is frequently justified through competitiveness, employability, and research connectivity (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). Those rationales intersect with distributional questions: EMI can function as gatekeeping when English proficiency, examinations, private preparation, and geography sort students before coursework begins (Piller, 2016; Macaro et al., 2018). EMI is therefore not only pedagogy but a structuring feature of opportunity in systems where the national language remains the default medium for most degrees.

In Türkiye, programme-level EMI growth and uneven disciplinary spread coexist with debates on internationalisation, prestige, and Turkish-medium provision (Altay, 2025; Curle et al., 2020). Despite a substantial body of work on language proficiency, academic achievement, and classroom processes after admission, comparatively little is known at national scale about whether **newly placed** EMI cohorts mirror the demographic and socioeconomic composition of Turkish-medium instruction (TMI), which remains the dominant undergraduate track. Without placement-level profiles, claims about equity, diversity, and inclusion (EDI) in access to EMI risk relying on institutional illustration or designs that do not generalise to the system.

This article addresses that gap by documenting how profiles of newly placed undergraduates in EMI and TMI differed in **2023–2025** on **gender**, **administratively recorded disability**, and **provincial socioeconomic tier**. Its contributions are threefold: **(1)** national **placement demography** contrasting EMI with TMI under a consistent content-EMI definition; **(2)** explicit attention to **disability representation** alongside gender and geography; and **(3)** tier-level patterns contextualised with **youth population denominators**, thereby reducing misinterpretation of raw counts in populous regions. The following sections review relevant scholarship, specify methods and data, present results, and discuss implications for policy and research.

## 2. Literature review

### 2.1 Equity, diversity, and inclusion in higher education and language education

Equity, diversity, and inclusion (EDI) have become central terms in higher-education policy and institutional self-description. Their analytical utility, however, depends on specifying what is being equalised, which differences matter, and inclusion into *what*—questions that connect policy language to structures of stratification in mass systems (Marginson, 2016; Ortaçtepe Hart, 2023). In practice, universities allocate scarce places, certify competence, and mediate labour-market signals; those functions are neither socially neutral nor evenly distributed across regions, languages, and bodies. Applied linguistics has long argued that language education is implicated in stratification: communicative repertoires interact with institutional norms about legitimacy, “standard” language, and academic voice (Piller, 2016). Ortaçtepe Hart (2023) synthesises resources for **social justice in the language classroom**, emphasising reflection, action, and transformation rather than treating instruction as a technical delivery problem. Related work on **expansive language access** argues that race, language, and dis/ability should be examined together across policy, programme, and practice, so that multiply marginalised learners are not addressed only through siloed administrative categories (Cioè-Peña et al., 2025). These perspectives establish EDI as a substantive research problem prior to any particular medium of instruction.

### 2.2 Internationalisation, EMI, and questions of access

Internationalisation agendas have intensified the spread of English-medium instruction (EMI) and reshaped the symbolic economy of degrees (Altbach & Knight, 2007; Dearden, 2014). Cross-national volumes document that EMI expansion raises common challenges—implementation variability, stakeholder readiness, and tensions between internationalisation rhetoric and local academic cultures—even as national responses diverge (Doiz et al., 2013; Dimova et al., 2015). Systematic reviews likewise underline heterogeneous EMI models and uneven evidence on language and content outcomes (Macaro et al., 2018). At the same time, research on **English as a lingua franca** in the international university stresses that choices about academic English are politically consequential: they can reproduce native-speakerist hierarchies or, alternatively, inform more plural norms of participation (Jenkins, 2014). Sociolinguistic accounts connect EMI to **linguistic justice**—who bears the costs of Englishisation, which languages count as academic, and whether multilingual repertoires are recognised (Piller, 2016; Soler, 2024). Empirical and conceptual contributions further link EMI programmes to **language rights** and structural inequity for users of English as an additional language (Alhamami, 2023). Critical EMI scholarship examines how Englishisation interacts with power, pedagogy, and institutional practice (Wang, 2025). Multilingual and planning-oriented responses propose that EMI need not collapse into monolingual English-only models if policy deliberately supports heterogeneous repertoires (Dafouz & Smit, 2022; Ou et al., 2022).

Taken together, this literature suggests that EMI should be studied as both **pedagogy** and **access structure**: proficiency thresholds, assessment formats, information flows, and regional programme supply can sort students before coursework begins. What remains less visible in many national systems is **administrative evidence on who obtains placement** in EMI relative to the default national-language track.

### 2.3 EMI in Türkiye: proficiency, geography, and the placement gap

In the Turkish context, English proficiency and prior educational pathways predict academic success in EMI after admission (Curle et al., 2020), and longitudinal work documents co-development of language proficiency and achievement during degree study (Yüksel et al., 2021; Soruç et al., 2021). Such evidence is indispensable for understanding experiences *inside* programmes, but it does not substitute for population-level description of **who enters** EMI versus Turkish-medium instruction (TMI) through central placement. Recent national argumentation further connects EMI supply and prestige to **linguistic protectionism** and uneven disciplinary geographies (Altay, 2025). The present study therefore treats TMI as the comparative baseline against which the inclusiveness of EMI at the **placement stage** can be assessed.

### 2.4 Operational definitions for this study

For this article, **equity** refers to fair access to pre-enrolment opportunity structures—preparation, examinations, information, and placement—not merely formal equality of rules. **Diversity** is operationalised through cohort composition on gender and administratively recorded disability; intersectional and non-binary identities may be under-represented in source metadata. **Inclusion** is examined at **entry**: whether EMI and TMI differ in who is counted among newly placed students prior to in-course accommodation. **Gender** comparisons address distributional balance across tracks; **disability** comparisons rely on recorded prevalence and are interpreted cautiously given disclosure and registration practices; **socioeconomic geography** uses provincial development tiers (SEGE-2025) with youth population denominators so that raw counts are not mistaken for proportional national reach. These definitions align the empirical analysis with the reviewed scholarship while acknowledging known limits of administrative data.

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Research questions

**RQ1.** How did the profile of newly placed undergraduate students in EMI and TMI programmes in Türkiye differ across the 2023–2025 admission cycles in terms of **(a)** gender, **(b)** disability, and **(c)** socioeconomic tier?

**RQ2.** How do students describe the role of gender, disability, and socioeconomic background in shaping access to EMI and TMI programmes in Türkiye?

### 3.2 Research aims and design

This study examines equity, diversity, and inclusion in Turkish higher education by investigating whether newly placed undergraduate students in English-medium instruction (EMI) and Turkish-medium instruction (TMI) programmes displayed different profiles across the 2023–2025 admission cycles. The analysis focuses on three dimensions closely related to access and representation: gender, disability, and socioeconomic tier. Within this framework, **TMI serves as the comparative baseline** against which the inclusiveness of EMI at the **placement stage** can be evaluated.

To address these aims, the study adopts an **explanatory sequential mixed-methods design**, in which quantitative analysis is followed by qualitative inquiry (Heigham & Croker, 2009; Riazi, 2016). The first research question requires **descriptive and comparative** analysis of national placement patterns; the second calls for **interpretive** exploration of how students understand the social factors shaping access to EMI and TMI. The quantitative phase establishes macro-level distributional patterns; the qualitative phase is intended to add explanatory depth by examining students’ perceptions and experiences. The two strands are treated as **complementary components** of an integrated design rather than as isolated methodological exercises.

### 3.3 Context of the study

Over the last two decades, EMI has expanded rapidly across higher education systems worldwide and has increasingly shifted from a relatively limited institutional practice to a major trend associated with globalisation, internationalisation, and academic competitiveness (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). As EMI has grown in scale and visibility, scholarly attention has moved beyond pedagogy and language proficiency to broader concerns related to access, participation, and social justice. EMI is now widely understood not only as a linguistic or curricular choice but also as a **policy and institutional phenomenon** with implications for equity, diversity, and inclusion.

Within the Turkish higher education context, this debate has become increasingly salient as EMI has continued to expand across universities and disciplinary fields. Recent work argues that EMI in Türkiye should be examined not merely through internationalisation and prestige, but also in relation to **access, inequality, and representation** within the national system (Altay, 2025; see also Curle et al., 2020; Yüksel et al., 2021, on proficiency and trajectories). Because TMI remains the **dominant mode** of undergraduate education, it furnishes a defensible comparative basis for asking whether EMI reflects a **similar student profile** or **more differentiated** patterns on gender, disability, and socioeconomic background. The present study therefore examines EMI and TMI comparatively through a social-justice lens.

### 3.4 Participants and units of analysis

**Quantitative phase.** The quantitative strand did not involve individual recruitment. The **unit of analysis** was the population of **newly placed undergraduate students** in EMI and TMI programmes in Türkiye for the **2023, 2024, and 2025** central admission cycles, as recorded in official placement statistics. “Newly placed” refers to students allocated to a degree programme through the national placement system for the corresponding cycle (operationalised via YÖK Atlas records for each year).

**Qualitative phase.** The interview strand involves **undergraduate students enrolled in EMI and TMI programmes**. Because RQ2 targets **interpretations of access**, participants were selected for their potential to illuminate programme choice, placement experience, and perceived opportunity structures. **Purposeful sampling** was used to maximise **relevant variation** across instructional medium and, where feasible, across gender, socioeconomic background, and disability-related experience (Dörnyei, 2001). Sampling proceeded **iteratively**, with data collection continuing until **thematic saturation** was approached—that is, until new interviews yielded no substantial elaboration of the emerging thematic structure (Dörnyei, 2001; Braun & Clarke, 2006).

### 3.5 Data collection

#### 3.5.1 Quantitative data sources and case definition

Quantitative data were obtained from **YÖK Atlas**, the official database of the Turkish Council of Higher Education (YÖK), which provides nationally standardised information on higher education programmes, institutions, and student placements. For this study, records were compiled for the **2023, 2024, and 2025** admission cycles and organised by **medium of instruction** at the undergraduate level, distinguishing **EMI** and **TMI** programmes.

**Conceptual boundary (content EMI).** In constructing the analytic dataset, **language-related programmes were excluded**. This follows the widely adopted definition of EMI as the teaching of **academic content** through English, rather than the teaching of **English as a subject** (Dearden, 2014; Macaro et al., 2018). Excluded programme families included, among others, English language studies, foreign language education, translation and interpreting, linguistics, and cognate language-centred fields. The purpose was to preserve **conceptual consistency** by restricting comparison to **content-based** instructional contexts. **Residual misclassification** (e.g., ambiguous or hybrid programme labels) was mitigated through manual review; a **small number** of records with incomplete or inconsistent fields were omitted **only** when reliable categorisation was not possible. The resulting dataset is best understood as a **curated national extract** optimised for **EMI–TMI comparability**, not an exhaustive census of every programme record in YÖK Atlas.

#### 3.5.2 Indicators and geographic–socioeconomic framework

Three **quantitative indicators** were employed:

1. **Gender distribution** of newly placed students, as recorded in administrative placement data (binary categories as reported by the source).
2. **Disability** among newly placed students: **counts and percentages** of students flagged as disabled in the placement records, compared across EMI and TMI.
3. **Socioeconomic geography**, operationalised through **provincial tier** membership using the **Research on the Socio-Economic Development Ranking of Provinces and Regions (SEGE-2025)**, published by the Ministry of Industry and Technology. SEGE-2025 evaluates provinces on **52 variables** organised under **eight thematic domains** and assigns each province to one of **six development tiers** (Tier 1 = highest development; Tier 6 = lowest in this scheme). Provinces were aggregated to tier level for comparison of placement distributions.

To interpret **raw placement counts** in light of the **underlying youth population** likely to supply university applicants, **denominators** were constructed from the **Population Statistics Portal** of the Turkish Statistical Institute (TURKSTAT), using the **Population by Age Group** interface. Because the portal reports **15–19** and **20–24** age bands separately, these were **combined** to approximate a **15–24** “candidate-age” pool at the provincial level, then **aggregated within each SEGE tier**. EMI and TMI placement counts were therefore examined both **absolutely** and **relative to tier-level 15–24 population**, yielding **population-adjusted placement intensities** by tier.

#### 3.5.3 Qualitative data collection

Qualitative data were collected through **semi-structured interviews**, chosen because they balance **topic coverage** with **space for participant-led elaboration** (Heigham & Croker, 2009). The protocol focused on how students understood the roles of **gender**, **disability**, and **socioeconomic background** in shaping access to EMI and TMI. It also explored perceived **opportunities and barriers**, **fairness**, **family influence**, **educational biography**, and the **symbolic meanings** students attach to each instructional pathway. Interviews were conducted in **Turkish or English** according to participant preference, **audio-recorded** with **informed consent**, and **transcribed verbatim** for analysis.

### 3.6 Analytic approach

#### 3.6.1 Quantitative analysis

The quantitative data were analysed using **descriptive statistics** and **comparative tabulations**. This choice reflects the study’s purpose: to document **patterns of representation** across EMI and TMI rather than to estimate **causal effects** of medium of instruction on individual outcomes. For each admission cycle, **frequencies** and **percentages** were computed for gender, disability, and socioeconomic tier; **cross-cycle stability** was assessed descriptively. For tier-level analysis, placements were related to **tier-aggregated 15–24 population** to contextualise magnitude. **Visual summaries** (e.g., tier profiles over time) were prepared to support transparent comparison between tracks (Agresti & Franklin, 2014; Field, 2013).

#### 3.6.2 Qualitative analysis

Interview transcripts were analysed using **Braun and Clarke’s (2006) reflexive thematic analysis** in six phases: (1) repeated reading for familiarisation; (2) **inductive coding**; (3) candidate theme generation; (4) theme review; (5) theme definition and naming; and (6) reporting. Coding emphasised participants’ **emic** accounts of access, inclusion, **exclusion**, and **structural constraint**. Analytic **credibility** was supported through **iterative comparison** between codes, themes, and original extracts; the final manuscript will present **illustrative quotations** where appropriate.

#### 3.6.3 Integration

In the **completed** mixed-methods write-up, quantitative results supply the **national backdrop** against which qualitative themes are interpreted; the interview strand is intended to explain **how** students **make sense of** the opportunity structures that the quantitative phase maps (Heigham & Croker, 2009). The present document reports the **quantitative strand in full** and reserves **integrated interpretation** for the qualitative subsection once analysis is finalised.

### 3.7 Ethics

The qualitative component was conducted in accordance with **institutional ethical review** **[insert protocol / approval number]**. Participants provided **informed consent**; confidentiality was protected; **withdrawal** was permitted; audio files and transcripts were stored **securely** with access limited to the research team. The quantitative analysis relied on **aggregated public administrative statistics**; **no individual-level student records** were obtained beyond what is already disseminated through YÖK Atlas and official demographic releases.

### 3.8 Scope, assumptions, and limitations (methodological)

Several boundaries condition interpretation and should be read alongside the Results:

- **Ecological socioeconomic measure.** SEGE tiers classify **provinces**, not students’ households. Tier membership is a **geographic proxy** for developmental context, not a direct measure of family income or parental education.
- **Administrative disability counts.** Disability prevalence depends on **registration practices**, **disclosure**, **legal/administrative categories**, and possible **under-reporting**. Comparisons indicate **recorded** representation, not clinical prevalence.
- **Binary gender metadata.** Source data did not support analysis of **non-binary** identities if not recorded.
- **Denominator approximation.** The 15–24 tier population is a **reasoned proxy** for the applicant pool, not a perfect match to the annual cohort sitting central examinations.
- **Aggregate EMI/TMI contrast.** The study does **not** disaggregate by **field of study** at this stage; observed contrasts may partly reflect **disciplinary composition** of EMI versus TMI supply in addition to medium *per se*.

Together, these points delimit **descriptive national evidence** on placement structure; they do **not** support causal claims that medium of instruction *causes* observed demographic differences.

## 4. Results

This section presents empirical findings in two stages. **First**, quantitative results address **RQ1** by describing how newly placed undergraduate students in EMI and TMI programmes in Türkiye differed across **2023–2025** on gender, disability, and socioeconomic tier. **Second**, qualitative findings for **RQ2**—students’ accounts of access—will be integrated in a subsequent revision once thematic analysis is complete. At present, the emphasis is on the **national quantitative profile** of EMI and TMI intakes.

In EMI, female students constituted **50.56%** (2023), **50.81%** (2024), and **50.71%** (2025) of newly placed students; male students accounted for **49.44%**, **49.19%**, and **49.29%**, respectively. TMI, by contrast, displayed a **more uneven** gender distribution. Thus TMI **consistently** showed a **pronounced female majority**.

In **absolute** terms, both tracks grew: EMI rose from **91,053** to **104,770** newly placed students; TMI from **427,274** to **492,179**. Despite this expansion, **gender composition remained remarkably stable** within each track, suggesting that scale-up between 2023 and 2025 did **not** materially re-shape the **gendered** composition of either pathway.

**Summary for RQ1a.** EMI and TMI **differed consistently** in gender distribution: EMI maintained **near parity**; TMI retained a **female-dominated** profile across all three years.

### 4.2 Distribution of newly placed disabled students across EMI and TMI

The second component of RQ1 concerns **disability** among newly placed students. As Table 1 shows, disabled students constituted a **very small fraction** of placements in **both** tracks. Nevertheless, a **consistent** gap appeared: **TMI recorded a slightly higher proportional share** than EMI in every cycle.

In EMI, disabled students accounted for **0.072%** (2023), **0.076%** (2024), and **0.073%** (2025), corresponding to **66**, **72**, and **77** students. Proportional representation remained **low and stable** despite modest **absolute** increases.

In TMI, the shares were **0.104%**, **0.102%**, and **0.097%**, with frequencies **446**, **460**, and **476**. Again, **absolute** counts rose slightly while **proportions** remained in a **narrow band**.

**Direct comparison** indicates that TMI **accommodated a slightly larger share** of **recorded** disabled students than EMI in each year. The difference is **small in percentage points** but **persistent**, suggesting that lower EMI representation was **not** a single-year artefact.

**Summary for RQ1b.** Disabled students were **under-represented** in administrative counts in **both** tracks, but **proportionally lower in EMI than in TMI** for **2023–2025**.

### 4.3 Socioeconomic-tier distribution across EMI and TMI

The third component of RQ1 addresses **socioeconomic geography**. Provincial membership in **SEGE-2025** tiers was used to classify placements; tier-level **15–24 population** denominators contextualised counts (full tier-by-year figures appear in **Appendix A**, Table 2; **Figure 1** summarises patterns visually in the submission version). This dual presentation allows assessment of both **absolute** placement volumes and **population-adjusted** penetration by tier.

The socioeconomic results reveal a **marked contrast** between EMI and TMI. Across all three years, **EMI placements were heavily concentrated in Tier 1** (the highest development tier). In **2023**, **55,905** of **91,053** newly placed EMI students (**≈61%**) were located in Tier 1; in **2024**, **59,300** of **94,823**; in **2025**, **66,648** of **104,770**. In substantive terms, roughly **three-fifths** of EMI entrants were drawn from the **most developed tier** throughout the window, and concentration **edged upward** slightly by 2025.

TMI also recorded its **largest absolute counts** in Tier 1, but the **distribution across tiers was more dispersed**. In **2023**, Tier 1 accounted for **166,334** of **427,274** TMI placements; in **2024**, **185,782** of **451,681**; in **2025**, **198,081** of **492,179**. Tier 1 remained the **modal** base for TMI, yet **concentration ratios** were **lower** than for EMI, indicating **greater spread** across middle and lower tiers.

The contrast intensifies in **lower tiers**. EMI placements in **Tier 6** numbered **2,810** (2023), **2,892** (2024), and **3,138** (2025). Expressed relative to Tier 6’s **15–24 population**, EMI penetration was **0.14%**, **0.15%**, and **0.16%**. TMI, by contrast, placed **54,700**, **53,049**, and **66,180** students in Tier 6—**far larger** in absolute terms.

**Middle tiers** show a **graded decline** in EMI population-adjusted rates from Tier 1 downward. For **2025**, EMI accounted for **1.30%** of the Tier 1 candidate-age pool, versus **0.74%** (Tier 2), **0.65%** (Tier 3), **0.45%** (Tier 4), **0.49%** (Tier 5), and **0.16%** (Tier 6). TMI’s tier profile was **less sharply peaked** at Tier 1: in **2025**, TMI reached **4.38%** of the Tier 4 pool and **4.23%** of Tier 5, compared with **3.86%** in Tier 1—illustrating **flatter** geographic penetration than EMI.

**Taken together**, EMI appears **more selective and spatially concentrated** in the **most developed** provinces, whereas TMI exhibits **broader national reach** when both **absolute** and **population-adjusted** perspectives are combined. EMI was **present** in all tiers, but its **footprint** in less-developed tiers—especially Tier 6—remained **limited** relative to both its Tier 1 mass and to TMI’s volumes.

**Temporal pattern.** From **2023** to **2025**, the **hierarchy** persisted: Tier 1 remained the **primary locus** of EMI placements; TMI retained a **more evenly spread** tier profile. Overall growth in placements **did not** erase these **structural** contrasts.

**Summary for RQ1c.** Newly placed EMI and TMI students were distributed across SEGE tiers in **systematically different** ways: EMI was **disproportionately concentrated** in Tier 1, while TMI showed **wider** dispersion across the **full** tier structure.

### 4.4 Summary of quantitative findings

Collectively, the quantitative results distinguish EMI and TMI as **non-equivalent** pathways by intake profile. EMI intakes were **more gender-balanced**; TMI intakes showed a **stable female majority**. **Recorded** disability shares were **very small** in both tracks but **consistently lower in EMI**. Most strikingly, EMI placements were **strongly concentrated** in the **highest socioeconomic tier**, whereas TMI displayed **broader** geographic distribution, including **higher population-adjusted presence** in several **middle** tiers and **far larger** volumes in **Tier 6**. These patterns are directly pertinent to **equity, diversity, and inclusion** monitoring in Turkish higher education.

### 4.5 Qualitative findings (RQ2)

Semi-structured interviews are being analysed using **reflexive thematic analysis** (Braun & Clarke, 2006) to examine how students narrate **gendered expectations**, **disability-related barriers and supports**, and **socioeconomic constraints** in accessing EMI and TMI. **This subsection will be completed** in a later revision; the quantitative results above establish the **national empirical context** within which those accounts will be situated.

## 5. Discussion

The results indicate stable, systematic differences between EMI and TMI in the composition of newly placed undergraduates between 2023 and 2025. EMI intakes were close to gender parity, whereas TMI intakes retained a female majority. Disability prevalence in administrative records was very low in both tracks but consistently lower in EMI. Socioeconomically, EMI placements were heavily concentrated in the highest SEGE tier, with comparatively limited population-adjusted penetration in the lowest tier; TMI showed broader dispersion across tiers. The persistence of these contrasts despite overall growth in placements supports interpreting them as structural features of the national intake pattern rather than as single-cycle anomalies.

These patterns are consistent with treating EMI as an element of a wider selection environment shaped by English proficiency demands, differential access to preparation, regional programme supply, and institutional signalling (Piller, 2016; Macaro et al., 2018; Alhamami, 2023). From a social-justice perspective on language education, EMI’s near parity on gender should not be equated with equity for all subgroups: disciplinary composition, pathway signalling, and the costs of pursuing English-heavy routes may still stratify access (Ortaçtepe Hart, 2023). The disability gap, although small in absolute terms, aligns with concerns that language-intensive gatekeeping and fragmented institutional responses can disadvantage multiply marginalised learners unless race, language, and dis/ability are addressed jointly across policy levels (Cioè-Peña et al., 2025; Alhamami, 2023). Follow-up analysis of student accounts may clarify mechanisms such as information constraints, disclosure, and perceptions of accommodation.

The socioeconomic gradient is the most pronounced finding. Tier-1 concentration of EMI, together with weak population-adjusted EMI rates in Tier 6, coheres with national accounts of EMI as intertwined with prestige economies and uneven supply (Altay, 2025) and with sociolinguistic arguments that English-medium routes are resource-intensive (Piller, 2016). Relative to much EMI research that emphasises institutional case studies or post-admission outcomes (Macaro et al., 2018; Curle et al., 2020; Yüksel et al., 2021), the present evidence foregrounds **placement-level demography** alongside Turkish-medium mass provision and makes disability visible at national scale. Linguistic justice and critical EMI perspectives caution against framing responses solely as “more English support” without attending to who bears the costs of Englishisation (Soler, 2024; Wang, 2025); multilingual planning perspectives suggest policy directions that recognise heterogeneous repertoires in EMI contexts (Dafouz & Smit, 2022; Ou et al., 2022). Ecological placement data cannot establish causal effects of medium of instruction; they nonetheless provide a transparent basis for monitoring access.

Interpretation is bounded by the limitations noted in Section 3.8: provincial tiers are ecological proxies; gender categories are binary in the source; disability figures reflect administrative recording; EMI and TMI are not disaggregated by field at this stage; individual proficiency is not observed in the extracts used here. Future research should examine discipline-stratified patterns, preparatory pathways, longer time horizons, and integration with interpretive evidence where available.

---

## 6. Conclusion

Between 2023 and 2025, newly placed students in **EMI** and **TMI** in Türkiye differed markedly in **gender balance**, **recorded disability representation**, and **socioeconomic geography**. EMI approached gender parity; TMI stayed female-majority. Disability shares were very small in both tracks and consistently lower in EMI. EMI placements clustered in the highest SEGE tier with limited population-adjusted presence in the lowest tier; TMI spread more broadly. These findings describe **who gains access** to each track at national scale and complement classroom- and proficiency-oriented EMI research (Macaro et al., 2018; Curle et al., 2020; Yüksel et al., 2021).

Contributions include **placement-level EDI evidence** for a major non-Anglophone system, **explicit disability visibility**, and a **replicable geographic method** (SEGE tiers with youth denominators). Interpreted alongside scholarship on social justice in language education and stratification in mass higher education (Ortaçtepe Hart, 2023; Piller, 2016; Marginson, 2016) and on expansive language access (Cioè-Peña et al., 2025), the findings support systematic **monitoring of inclusion** as EMI expands (Altay, 2025). Linguistic justice and language-rights perspectives further highlight distributional consequences of Englishisation (Soler, 2024; Alhamami, 2023). Complementary qualitative evidence may connect these distributions to student meanings of fairness and constraint.

**Implications (hypothesis-generating):** widen EMI-related **outreach and preparation** in lower-tier regions; review **English-intensive gatekeeping** and **disability-inclusive** admissions and support; maintain **gender-aware** guidance where TMI pathways are highly feminised. Evidence on **what works** should combine these structural indicators with evaluation of specific interventions.
